Great—this is exactly the right move strategically.

I’ll give you a **long, usable list of “safe” public domain stories**, meaning:

* minimal violence
* minimal gender / ideological issues
* adaptable for modern children’s books
* clearly public domain (mostly from Aesop, Jacob Grimm & Wilhelm Grimm, Hans Christian Andersen, and global folklore collections) ([No Film School][1])

---

# 🟢 1. VERY SAFE (best category overall)

👉 Short, moral, animal-based stories (ideal for children’s books)

### Aesop-style fables (huge goldmine)

* The Tortoise and the Hare
* The Lion and the Mouse
* The Fox and the Grapes
* The Ant and the Grasshopper
* The Crow and the Pitcher
* The Dog and His Reflection
* The Town Mouse and the Country Mouse
* The Goose That Laid the Golden Eggs
* The Bundle of Sticks
* The Wind and the Sun
* The Milkmaid and Her Pail
* The Fox and the Crow
* The Wolf in Sheep’s Clothing
* The Frog and the Ox
* The Two Goats
* The Stag and His Reflection
* The Fox and the Stork
* The Dog in the Manger
* The Hare and His Ears
* The Eagle and the Beetle

👉 There are **100+ of these**, all public domain and widely used in education ([Read.gov][2])

---

# 🟢 2. SAFE ANIMAL / HUMOR FOLK TALES

👉 Slightly more narrative, still low-risk

* The Bremen Town Musicians
* The Elves and the Shoemaker
* The Little Red Hen
* The Cat and the Mouse
* The Three Sillies
* The Husband Who Was to Mind the House
* The Old Woman and Her Pig
* The Hobyahs
* The Half Chick (Medio Pollito)
* Lambikin (Indian folktale)
* Epaminondas (humor folktale)

👉 These come from Grimm, English, Indian, and Norse traditions ([Project Gutenberg][3])

---

# 🟢 3. SAFE MAGIC / FANTASY STORIES (LOW CONTROVERSY)

👉 Good for narrative + imagination, without problematic themes

### From Grimm & European folklore

* The Golden Goose
* The White Snake
* The Twelve Brothers
* The Three Spinners
* Faithful John (can be softened)
* The Story of a Youth Who Went Forth to Learn What Fear Was
* The Golden Bird
* Mother Hulda (softened versions)

### From Andersen (generally more philosophical but adaptable)

* The Emperor’s New Clothes
* The Nightingale
* The Tinderbox
* The Flying Trunk
* The Steadfast Tin Soldier (slightly sad but usable)

👉 These are part of large 19th-century collections now public domain ([Wikipedia][4])

---

# 🟢 4. SAFE GLOBAL FOLK TALES (excellent for originality)

👉 Often underused + culturally rich

### Middle Eastern / Arabic

* Aladdin (adapt carefully, avoid specific adaptations)
* Sinbad the Sailor (episodic adventures—choose lighter ones)

### Asian

* Momotarō (Japan)
* The Bamboo Cutter (Japan – adapt gently)
* The Dragon Painter (China)

### European / Norse

* East of the Sun and West of the Moon (softened)
* The Husband Who Was to Mind the House

👉 Large global collections exist with hundreds of such tales ([Duke University Library Guides][5])

---

# 🟢 5. SAFE “LIGHT” FAIRY TALES (WITH MINOR ADAPTATION)

👉 Recognizable but need slight rewriting

* Goldilocks and the Three Bears
* Jack and the Beanstalk (reduce violence)
* The Frog Prince (remove coercion elements)
* Thumbelina (mostly safe)
* The Snow Queen (select parts)

👉 These are commonly reused and adapted freely ([whileshenaps.com][6])

---

# 🟢 6. SAFE NONSENSE / PLAYFUL STORIES

👉 Very good for modern children’s tone

* The Owl and the Pussycat
* The Jumblies
* Noodlehead stories (foolish logic tales)

👉 These are especially good if you want humor or absurdity

---

# 🧠 Hidden gem category (very valuable)

👉 “Noodlehead” stories (worldwide tradition)

These are:

* funny
* illogical
* philosophical in a subtle way

Examples:

* The Three Sillies
* Foolish villagers stories
* Trickster misunderstandings

👉 These are extremely adaptable and **rarely controversial**
