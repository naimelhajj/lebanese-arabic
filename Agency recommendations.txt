APPROVED & FINAL ACTION PLAN
----------------------------

1.  **Unify the Call to Action (CTA):**
    *   Delete the red "Claim Your $15 Trial Lesson" button.
    *   Delete the text link "Start with a $15 Trial Lesson".
    *   The page must have one single, unambiguous primary CTA button.

2.  **Streamline Page Flow:**
    *   Delete the entire "Choose Your Path" section. It is a premature and confusing distraction.

3.  **Reduce Page Length:**
    *   Modify the FAQ section so that all accordion items are closed by default.

No other changes will be made to the page. Upon completion of these tweaks, the "Tip of the Spear" offensive will commence immediately.

""" Protocol Activated: "The 'Refactoring UI' Design Critic"

  My analysis is based on objective design principles that maximize clarity and conversion.

   1. Color & CTA Conflict: You have three different colors for primary actions: green (#2d7a6a), red

      Your..." button is especially problematic as it clashes with the brand's established green and is

   2. Visual Inconsistency: As we diagnosed, the "Pay as You Go" and "Best Value" elements are styled

       visual language in a critical part of the page.
   3. Redundant Navigation: The "Choose Your Path" section with its two large, green CTA-style buttons
      ("See Prepared Prices," "See Conversational Prices") adds significant visual noise and a
      confusing, premature choice for the user. It distracts from the single goal of this page.

  ---

  Protocol Activated: "The 'Nielsen' UX/UI Advocate"

  My focus is on removing friction from the user's journey.

   1. Multiple Confusing CTAs: A user sees a green "Book..." button, a red "Claim..." button, and a
      text link "Start with a...". This violates Heuristic #4 (Consistency) and forces the user to
      waste mental energy deciding if these are different offers. This is a high-friction interaction.
   2. Information Overload: For a direct-response page targeting high-intent users, the page is
      excessively long. The FAQ section, while useful, is fully expanded by default, making the page
      feel like a marathon scroll.
   3. False Choices: The "Choose Your Path" section presents a choice that is irrelevant at this stage.
       The user is here for a trial. Forcing them to consider "Prepared" vs. "Conversational" before
      they've even signed up adds cognitive load and decision fatigue.

  ---

  Protocol Activated: "The 'Inversion' Marketing Strategist"
